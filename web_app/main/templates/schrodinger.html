The Schrodinger Equation

The Evolution of Wavefunctions

The Schrodinger equation is a very famous equation that describes the evolution of systems in time. More precisely, it describes how a wavefunction changes in time when it is inside a Hamiltonian (ie. a potential). The equation with time dependence is typically quoted as follows: \[ i \hbar \frac{d}{d t}|\Psi(t)\rangle=\hat{H}|\Psi(t)\rangle \] We can break it down into its parts like so:

  • The left hand side of the equation \( i \hbar \frac{d}{d t}|\Psi(t)\rangle \) represents the derivative in time the wavefunction \( |\Psi(t)\rangle \) (recall Bra-Ket notation from the previous sec.). Note that this may be a complex quantity.
  • The right hand side of the equation, involves how the Hamiltonian \( \hat{H} \) acts on the wavefunction \( |\Psi(t)\rangle \). This encapsulates the description of the energy of the system.
The time-dependent Schrodinger equation says that the way in which the wavefunction \( |\Psi(t)\rangle \) evolves in time is given by the energy of the given wavefunction in the system. This is analogous to an equation of motion in classical mechanics, however, we only use the first derivative of the wavefunction.

LC potentials

We can describe the evolution of LC circuits with the following Hamiltonian: \[ H = \frac{Q^2}{2C} + \frac{\Phi^2}{2L} \] Here we use \(Q\) to represent the charge in the circuit and \(\Phi\) to represent the magnetic flux in the circuit. \(C\) and \(L\) represent the capacitance and the inductance in the circuit. To turn this into a quantum mechanical Hamiltonian we denote the quantities \(Q\) and \(\Phi\) as operators \(\hat{Q}\) and \(\hat{\Phi}\). Now with this Hamiltonian we can see how the wavefunctions evolve.

  2. The needle on a car spedometer is loose and free to swing. When you flick it, it is equally likely to land between \(0\) and \(\pi\) and can bounce off a pin at \(3\pi/2\).
    1. What is the probability density \(\rho(\theta)\)? Graph it as a function of \(\theta\) from \(-\pi/2\) to \(3\pi/2\). Hint: \(\rho(x)\) is 0 in a section of the interval. Make sure that the total probability is 1.
    2. Compute \(⟨\theta⟩\), \(⟨\theta⟩^2\) and \(\sigma\) for the distribution
  3. Consider the following probability density function: \[\rho(x)=A e^{-\lambda(x-a)^{2}}\]
    1. Determine \(A\)
    2. Compute \(⟨x⟩\), \(⟨x⟩^2\) and \(\sigma\)
    3. Sketch the graph of \(\rho(x)\)
  4. Consider the following function named \(\Psi(x,t)\). We define \(|\Psi(x,t)|^2 =\Psi(x,t) \times \Psi(x,t)= \rho(x,t)\). At \(t=0\): \[\Psi(x, 0)=\left\{\begin{array}{ll} A x / a, & \text { if } 0 \leq x \leq a, \\ A(b-x) /(b-a), & \text { if } a \leq x \leq b, \\ 0, & \text { otherwise } \end{array}\right.\]
    1. Find \(A\) in terms of \(a\) and \(b\) to ensure normalization
    2. What is the expectation value of \(x\) at \(t=0\)?
